The engine: a closed cycle over reservoirs

A live reservoir computer you can perturb. The memory below is stored nowhere in particular: the whole system is the reservoir, only the readout is trained, and remembering is reconstruction. Sever the loop and watch what "memory" degrades into.

An interactive essay · follows Vol. I, "Spreading activation" Read time · 6 min

Volume I treated memory as a network of stored nodes waking one another. This model throws away the last comforting assumption: that anything is stored at all. In its place, an engine. A stream of experience pours in, heavy with token noise. A background consolidation process runs continuously, pulling the stream down, stripping the noise, and training a single component, the readout, against the whole rippling system. When you remember, nothing is fetched. The readout runs, and the moment is rebuilt from the current state of the pond.

In reservoir-computing terms (the frame Michael Levin borrows), the whole system is the reservoir and only the readout is trained. Three claims follow, and each one is visible in the model below:

  • Consolidation trains it; reconstruction runs it. The same readout, two modes. Training never stops, because the world never stops drifting.
  • There is no single store being written. The memory lives in the joint state of every unit at once. Delete any one unit and nothing in particular is lost.
  • The loop is what makes it memory. Reconstruction feeds action, action changes the world, the world feeds the stream, and the self re-gates the next pass. Cut that cycle anywhere and you are left with a recording, not a memory.

§ 01The model

Below is a real reservoir network, 90 units with fixed random recurrent connections, running live in your browser. A drifting world signal, mixed with a distractor and noise, streams into it. An online learning rule (the consolidation process) continuously trains a linear readout to reconstruct the clean world signal from the reservoir's state. The readout weights are the only thing that ever learns. Everything else just ripples.

§ 02What to look for

  1. Wipe the readout. Press the button. The reservoir keeps rippling exactly as before, yet reconstruction collapses to a flat line and error spikes. The ripples alone are not the memory. Memory is ripples plus a trained way of reading them. Consolidation retrains the readout from scratch in seconds; watch the red line find the black one again.
  2. Pause consolidation. Toggle it off and wait. Nothing breaks immediately; the readout keeps running fine. Then, as the world drifts, error creeps up and never comes back down. Reconstruction runs without training, but not for long, because the world will not hold still. This is why the background process never finishes. There is no "done" state for memory.
  3. Close the gate. Slide the self-gate toward zero. The stream is still full volume, but now it carries the distractor instead of the world. Error climbs even though consolidation is working perfectly. The self decides what the engine gets to learn from. Gating is not a filter on quantity; it selects which world the readout ends up tuned to.
  4. Sever the loop. Toggle the loop open. Action no longer stabilizes the world, so the world wanders and lurches. Consolidation chases it, but error stays high and jumpy. Closed, the system acts on the world it remembers and keeps that world predictable enough to remember. Severed, it becomes a recording device pointed at chaos. The closure is the memory.

§ 03The load-bearing claim

Draw the whole engine and one feature stands out: the self is not a station on the cycle. It sits at the hub, coupled to every stage by spokes that point both ways.

Those orange spokes are the load-bearing claim of the whole model. The self gates the stream, deciding what the engine learns from next, so the self shapes memory. But consolidation is continuously retraining the readout the self depends on, so memory shapes the self right back. Neither direction has priority. There is no version of you that exists before the loop and merely uses it.

The self both shapes memory and is shaped by it. What makes it memory rather than a store is that the loop closes.

This is why "where is the memory stored?" is the wrong question in this frame. Ask instead: what keeps the cycle turning, and what would it cost to cut it? Experiment 04 above is the answer in miniature. A severed loop does not lose its data; it loses its grip on the world the data was about.

§ 04What this is and isn't

Unlike Volume I's hand-tuned animation, the reservoir above is the real thing: fixed sparse random recurrent weights scaled near the edge of stability, leaky nonlinear units, and a linear readout trained by an online least-squares rule. That is a genuine (if tiny) echo state network, and the first three experiments demonstrate genuine properties of it. Honest caveats, as before:

First, the loop-closure coupling is a cartoon. In the model, "action stabilizes the world" is implemented directly: with the loop closed, the world's drift is damped; severed, it wanders and takes occasional jumps. Real agents stabilize their worlds through behavior (you keep your keys in the same bowl precisely so that remembering works). The mechanism here asserts that effect rather than earning it. The claim it illustrates is the model's, not the simulation's.

Second, the learning rule is not biology. Normalized least-mean-squares is a stand-in for whatever consolidation actually is, which runs on multiple timescales, does much of its work offline during sleep, and involves replay rather than a continuous error signal. What survives the simplification: training is a background process over the whole reservoir, and it never terminates.

Third, 90 units is not a mind. A reservoir this small holds one drifting signal. The model this page illustrates proposes the same architecture at the scale of a life: the stream is everything that happens to you, and the readout is everything you can rebuild from it.

Reservoir computing was formalized twice, independently: Herbert Jaeger's echo state networks (2001) and Wolfgang Maass's liquid state machines (2002). The framing borrowed here, in which a biological system is treated as a reservoir with a trained readout, and the self as the gating hub of a closed sensing-acting cycle, follows Michael Levin's work on memory and agency. The claim that remembering is reconstruction rather than retrieval goes back to Frederic Bartlett (1932) and is echoed in the modern reconsolidation literature.
